How a hub works

Layer 1 · Physical Intelligence: none Collision domain: shared

A hub is an electrical repeater. It has no idea what a MAC address is — whatever bits arrive on one port are simply copied out to every other port, at the same time.

Because every device shares the same electrical segment, only one PC can transmit at a moment without corrupting another signal. If two send together, their signals collide and both frames are destroyed — the reason hubs were retired in favor of switches.

    How a switch works

    Layer 2 · Data Link Intelligence: MAC learning Collision domain: per port

    A switch reads the source MAC address of every incoming frame and records which port it arrived on — that's the MAC address table (also called a CAM table) shown below.

    When it needs to forward a frame, it checks the table for the destination's MAC. Unknown yet? It floods, just like a hub. Already learned? It sends the frame straight to that one port — no one else even sees it.

      How a modem works

      Layer 1 · Physical Full name: MOdulator‑DEModulator Job: signal conversion

      Each PC only speaks in digital square waves — clean 0s and 1s. Telephone and cable lines were built to carry continuous analog waves, not sharp digital pulses, so a modem sits between every PC and that line.

      When PC 1 sends data to PC 2, Modem 1 modulates PC 1's digital bits onto an analog carrier wave that travels to the ISP. The ISP receives and relays that signal onward to Modem 2, which demodulates it back into digital bits for PC 2. Sending the other way just reverses the roles — Modem 2 modulates, the ISP relays, and Modem 1 demodulates.
